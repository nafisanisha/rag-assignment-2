History is the study of people, societies, events, and changes that happened in the past. Historians use evidence to understand how people lived, how governments developed, why conflicts occurred, and how present conditions were created. History is not only a collection of dates. It also explains causes, effects, ideas, and human experiences.

Primary sources are materials created during the historical period being studied. Examples include letters, diaries, laws, photographs, speeches, newspapers, buildings, and artifacts. Secondary sources are later interpretations, such as textbooks, research articles, and documentaries.

Historians evaluate sources carefully. They ask who created the source, when it was created, why it was created, and whether it contains bias. A source may provide useful evidence even if it is incomplete or biased. Comparing several sources can create a more balanced understanding.

Ancient civilizations developed cities, governments, writing systems, trade networks, laws, agriculture, and religious traditions. Civilizations grew in different regions and adapted to their environments. Trade routes connected societies and exchanged goods, technologies, languages, beliefs, and diseases.

Empires controlled large territories and different groups of people. They often expanded through military power, political control, and economic influence. Empires could create transportation networks and shared systems, but they could also cause exploitation, inequality, and resistance.

Industrialization changed production by increasing the use of machines and factories. It encouraged urban growth and created new employment, but workers often faced difficult conditions. Industrialization also increased pollution and changed family life, transportation, communication, and international trade.

Colonialism involved powerful countries controlling other territories and people. Its political, economic, cultural, and social effects often continued after independence. Studying history helps people evaluate evidence, understand different perspectives, identify long-term changes, and recognize how past decisions influence the present.

Migration has played an important role in world history. People have moved because of trade, employment, war, persecution, climate, and natural disasters. Migration can spread languages, skills, beliefs, food, and technology. It can also create conflict when resources or political rights are unequal.

Revolutions produce major political or social changes. They may begin because of inequality, economic problems, political oppression, new ideas, or weak government. Revolutions can create new institutions, but they can also cause violence and instability. Historians examine both the goals of revolutionary leaders and the experiences of ordinary people.

Historical interpretations may change when new evidence is discovered or when researchers ask different questions. Two historians may study the same event but emphasize different causes or groups. A strong historical explanation should use reliable evidence, acknowledge limitations, and explain why a particular interpretation is reasonable.
