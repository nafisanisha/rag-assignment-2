Public health focuses on protecting and improving the health of communities and populations. It includes disease prevention, health education, research, environmental protection, policy, and organized health services. Public health tries to prevent health problems before they become serious.

Disease prevention includes vaccination, screening, sanitation, safe drinking water, healthy food, physical activity, and infection control. Vaccination prepares the immune system to recognize particular diseases. High vaccination coverage can reduce the spread of infection within a community.

Sanitation means the safe management of human waste, wastewater, and environmental cleanliness. Poor sanitation can contaminate water and food and spread diseases. Safe drinking water, toilets, waste collection, and handwashing facilities are basic public-health needs.

Epidemiology is the study of how diseases and health conditions are distributed in populations. Epidemiologists examine who is affected, where cases occur, and when they happen. This information helps identify risk factors and possible causes.

Public-health surveillance continuously collects and analyzes health information. It can help detect an outbreak early. During an outbreak investigation, health workers identify cases, interview affected people, collect samples, study patterns, and recommend control measures.

Health education provides people with clear information about health risks and healthy behavior. Effective messages should be accurate, understandable, practical, and appropriate for the community. Public health also addresses nutrition, maternal health, child health, mental health, workplace safety, and environmental risks.

Health is influenced by social determinants such as income, education, housing, employment, transport, food access, and social support. Health equity means that everyone should have a fair opportunity to achieve good health. Public-health decisions should use evidence, protect privacy, consider ethics, and respond to the needs of different communities.

Nutrition is an important part of public health. A balanced diet supports growth, learning, immunity, and long-term health. Poor nutrition can include both insufficient food and unhealthy food choices. Public-health programs may support food safety, child nutrition, school meals, and education about healthy diets.

Mental health is also part of overall health. Stress, isolation, poverty, violence, and difficult working conditions can affect mental well-being. Communities need accessible services, accurate information, and reduced stigma. Early support can prevent some problems from becoming more serious.

Emergency preparedness helps communities respond to epidemics, floods, storms, heatwaves, and other disasters. Preparedness includes trained workers, communication plans, medical supplies, emergency shelters, and cooperation among organizations. After an emergency, public-health teams monitor disease, water safety, food access, and the needs of vulnerable groups.
